For most of the history of software, it was generally understood that the
most valuable part of a program is its technical or computational logic —
the backend. This assumption has become increasingly outdated. Applications
of all kinds, especially web and mobile apps, are now designed heavily
around the user interface (UI) and user experience (UX) — collectively,
the frontend. There are now entire professions dedicated to these areas,
and the work they produce is more comparable to art than to traditional
software engineering.

Creative works and software have long been treated as separate domains
under the law. This license rejects that distinction. Code that expresses a
visual idea, an interaction, or an experience is a creative work — and
its authors deserve the same recognition the law affords to any other
artist. It is perhaps surprising, then, that there are generally very few
legal mechanisms to protect the work of frontend developers, especially
when their work is integrated into a larger software project whose
governing license offers little protection. This opens the door for large
corporations to take advantage of such work — minimally modifying it,
presenting it as their own, and offering no visible acknowledgment to their
end users of where it originated.

Princess Evelyn's License is designed to prevent this kind of misuse. This
is not a requirement to promote or advertise. It is simply the recognition
that erasing an author's contribution — presenting their creative work as
your own to the people who use it — is a form of misappropriation that no
free license should permit. Accordingly, this license requires attribution
where it is clearly due: where the expressive, frontend work of the
original authors is visible to end users. It places no such requirement on
